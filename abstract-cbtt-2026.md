# Extended abstract — CBTT Research Review Seminar 2026

**Submission for:** Research Review Seminar 2026, "Operating in a Fractured World: Economic Resilience, Innovation and Sustainability," Central Bank of Trinidad and Tobago, 10 September 2026 (virtual presentation).

**Sub-theme:** 4. Macroeconomic Policy and Stability (with relevance to 2. Climate and Environmental Resilience).

**Title:** Expanding the risk financing toolkit in the Dutch Caribbean: sovereign and non-sovereign risk transfer under the Kingdom of the Netherlands' financial-supervision architecture.

**Author:** Rendell de Kort, Cornerstone Economics and Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam. rendell@c-economics.com.

---

In a fractured world the question for a small, open, tourism-dependent island economy is not whether external shocks will arrive but how the response is financed, and with how much policy autonomy. This paper examines how fixed exchange-rate central banks in the Caribbean answer that question, and why the four central-bank jurisdictions of the Dutch Caribbean answer it so differently from their neighbours. It takes as its starting point a fact that is rarely named in domestic debate: several of these central banks hold foreign reserves above the levels international adequacy standards require to defend the peg, while engaging only marginally with the disaster risk financing instruments that have matured over the past fifteen years. Reserves are the most expensive single instrument against which to hold marginal capital when complements exist, and the paper asks what the fuller toolkit offers and why so little of it is used.

The analysis compares five jurisdictions selected to hold institutional mandate roughly constant, all fixed-peg island economies: the Eastern Caribbean Currency Union, Barbados, the Bahamas, and the two Dutch Caribbean central banks (Aruba; Curaçao and Sint Maarten). Reserve adequacy is measured from the most recent IMF Article IV consultations, the risk-financing toolkit is set out as a risk-layering stack (retention and reserves, contingent credit, parametric insurance, capital-market transfer), and the paper reads two live cases in depth: Sint Maarten's sovereign parametric coverage and its emerging Disaster Reserve Fund, and Aruba's use of off-balance-sheet public-private vehicles.

Four findings follow. First, the toolkit available to the Dutch Caribbean is truncated from the top. The Kingdom's financial-supervision architecture forecloses the debt-based and market-based layers, because the constituent countries are not sovereign borrowing members of the World Bank or the Inter-American Development Bank and cannot access the catastrophe deferred drawdown options, contingent credit facilities, or catastrophe-bond markets that their sovereign neighbours use. What remains reachable is risk transfer that creates no debt, parametric insurance, and self-financed reserves. Second, with the external contingent-credit layer closed, risk finance is pushed off the sovereign balance sheet into corporatized vehicles: Sint Maarten is capitalising a Disaster Reserve Fund from the reflows of a loan to its airport company, and Aruba financed roughly 1.7 billion guilders of infrastructure through off-balance-sheet structures that the financial supervisor later reclassified into the debt quota. The two cases are one mechanism, and the paper specifies the point at which supervisory reclassification bites, namely debt-creating (securitised) structures rather than reserve-plus-insurance ones. Third, where the toolkit is reachable and unused, the binding constraint is institutional and relational rather than statutory, a claim the paper evidences from the asymmetry inside the IMF surveillance record itself and from the 2025 cluster of Caribbean catastrophe deferred drawdown options, whose supply-side logic structurally excluded the Dutch Caribbean. Fourth, pre-arranged instruments function as autonomy infrastructure relative to the conditionality bundled with emergency Kingdom transfers, demonstrated through the COVID-era financing negotiations, with a recipient-side mirror in earmarked, capture-resistant payouts.

The paper connects directly to the seminar theme. It reads the 2024-2026 retreat from climate-labelled finance, the United States' withdrawal from climate commitments and the resulting pressure that led the World Bank to retire its climate-lending targets in June 2026, and argues that disaster risk financing survives this fragmentation because it is already framed as fiscal-resilience and public-finance management rather than as climate policy. The fiscal-resilience framing is the durable one for a fractured world, and the paper holds it with the critical tension that such depoliticisation also displaces distributional claims.

The policy contribution is a portfolio framing for Caribbean central banks and finance ministries: matching each layer of risk to the cheapest instrument that can bear it lowers the aggregate cost of resilience relative to reserve accumulation alone, and for the Dutch Caribbean specifically, closing the gap requires either a change in how the Kingdom intermediates multilateral access for its constituent countries, or the self-financed, supervisor-scrutinised substitutes now being assembled.

**Method and data:** comparative institutional analysis; IMF Article IV staff reports; World Bank and CCRIF instrument documentation; the author's co-authored World Bank disaster-risk-finance assessment for Sint Maarten; the Aruban Court of Audit record. **Format:** virtual presentation via Microsoft Teams.
